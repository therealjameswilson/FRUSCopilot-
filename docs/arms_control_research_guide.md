# FRUS Arms Control Research Guide (Published Volumes + Declassified Source Targets)

This guide is designed for a FRUS compiler working on an **in-progress arms control volume** and wanting fast orientation to already published volumes on `history.state.gov`, plus concrete declassified-document hunting targets across U.S. government repositories.

## 1) Published FRUS Arms-Control-Related Volumes to Mine First

## A. FRUS, 1964–1968, Volume XI, *Arms Control and Disarmament*
- Volume landing page: https://history.state.gov/historicaldocuments/frus1964-68v11
- Why this matters:
  - Establishes baseline U.S. strategy on nonproliferation and superpower arms limitation prior to the Nixon period.
  - Useful for tracing continuity/discontinuity in ACDA, State, and White House negotiating positions.

Suggested key document entry points:
- Document 1 (editorial framing/start of collection): https://history.state.gov/historicaldocuments/frus1964-68v11/d1
- Mid-volume sample document: https://history.state.gov/historicaldocuments/frus1964-68v11/d50

## B. FRUS, 1969–1976, Volume XXXII, *SALT I, 1969–1972*
- Volume landing page: https://history.state.gov/historicaldocuments/frus1969-76v32
- Why this matters:
  - Core record for negotiating logic behind the ABM Treaty and Interim Agreement.
  - Shows how verification, throw-weight concerns, and MIRV politics were handled at high level.

Suggested key document entry points:
- Document 1: https://history.state.gov/historicaldocuments/frus1969-76v32/d1
- NSC-level negotiation context sample: https://history.state.gov/historicaldocuments/frus1969-76v32/d92
- Endgame/implementation period sample: https://history.state.gov/historicaldocuments/frus1969-76v32/d203

## C. FRUS, 1969–1976, Volume XXXIII, *SALT II, 1972–1980*
- Volume landing page: https://history.state.gov/historicaldocuments/frus1969-76v33
- Why this matters:
  - Essential for limits of détente and why ratification politics diverged from negotiating outcomes.
  - Tracks unresolved verification and compliance debates that echo in later strategic arms talks.

Suggested key document entry points:
- Document 1: https://history.state.gov/historicaldocuments/frus1969-76v33/d1
- Core negotiation sample: https://history.state.gov/historicaldocuments/frus1969-76v33/d74
- Late-period political/ratification context sample: https://history.state.gov/historicaldocuments/frus1969-76v33/d249

## D. FRUS, 1977–1980, Volume VI, *Soviet Union*
- Volume landing page: https://history.state.gov/historicaldocuments/frus1977-80v06
- Why this matters:
  - Not a dedicated arms control volume, but often critical for SALT II diplomatic context, alliance messaging, and strategy toward Moscow.

Suggested key document entry points:
- Document 1: https://history.state.gov/historicaldocuments/frus1977-80v06/d1
- Carter-era strategic diplomacy sample: https://history.state.gov/historicaldocuments/frus1977-80v06/d119

## 2) Theme Map for “Sequence-Aware” Research

When reading published volumes in sequence, use this crosswalk:

1. **Doctrine to Negotiation Architecture**
   - Early volumes: conceptual U.S. goals (stability, parity language, strategic sufficiency).
   - Later volumes: treaty text mechanics, ceilings/subceilings, and counting rules.

2. **Verification and Intelligence Dependence**
   - Recurrent concern: what can be reliably verified by national technical means.
   - Key for compiler notes: where intelligence confidence levels directly shape negotiating fallback positions.

3. **Interagency Friction and Bureaucratic Signatures**
   - ACDA vs DoD vs NSC vs State policy emphasis often visible in memoranda language and option papers.
   - Track repeated principals and drafting chains to identify “institutional voice.”

4. **Congressional/Domestic Ratification Environment**
   - Especially prominent in SALT II materials.
   - Useful for annotation when treaty outcomes diverge from executive branch negotiating goals.

5. **Alliance Signaling and Soviet Theater Linkages**
   - NATO consultation, European concerns, and linkage with regional crises often appear in supposedly bilateral strategic records.

## 3) Declassified Document Targets (Federal Websites + National Security Archive)

Below are concrete repositories and high-yield entry pages to source declassified records that can complement FRUS editorial coverage.

## A. U.S. Department of State FOIA
- Main portal: https://foia.state.gov
- Electronic Reading Room landing: https://foia.state.gov/Search/Collections.aspx
- FRUS compilers should target:
  - Lot Files
  - INR intelligence memoranda
  - Office of the Historian-related released compilations where available

## B. Presidential Libraries (NARA)
Use library-specific digital collections/search pages for NSC files, presidential correspondence, and memoranda of conversation.

- Nixon Library: https://www.nixonlibrary.gov
- Ford Library: https://www.fordlibrarymuseum.gov
- Carter Library: https://www.jimmycarterlibrary.gov
- Reagan Library: https://www.reaganlibrary.gov

Research tactic:
- Search for the same date window and participants identified in FRUS memoranda of conversation to locate additional drafts, backchannel messages, and annotation copies.

## C. CIA Records Search Tool (CREST) / CIA Reading Room
- CIA Reading Room main page: https://www.cia.gov/readingroom/
- CREST search landing: https://www.cia.gov/readingroom/search/site

High-value document types:
- National Intelligence Estimates (NIEs)
- Strategic force assessments tied to verification claims
- Senior-level intelligence briefings prepared for arms control talks

## D. National Archives (NARA)
- Main page: https://www.archives.gov
- Catalog search: https://catalog.archives.gov

High-yield record groups for arms control projects often include:
- RG 59 (Department of State General Records)
- NSC institutional files (via presidential library holdings)
- Defense and JCS policy files relevant to treaty options

## E. Additional Federal Sources Worth Using

### Office of the Historian (State) policy pages
- Office landing: https://history.state.gov
- Historical Documents landing: https://history.state.gov/historicaldocuments

### Department of Defense FOIA / reading-room materials
- DoD FOIA portal: https://www.esd.whs.mil/FOID/

### Government Publishing Office / Congressional record support
- GovInfo: https://www.govinfo.gov
- Use for hearings, treaty debates, and ratification-era statements.

## F. National Security Archive (George Washington University)
- Main page: https://nsarchive.gwu.edu
- Nuclear/arms collections often include briefing books and document sets with independent annotations and source citations.

Research tactic:
- Use NSA briefing books to identify document titles and archival callouts, then pull the same item from federal repositories when possible for citation robustness.

## 4) Practical Workflow for a Compiler

1. Start with one anchor published volume (e.g., SALT I), and build a table with columns:
   - Date
   - Sender/recipient
   - Negotiation issue
   - Verification relevance
   - Follow-on lead (State FOIA / library / CIA / NARA / NSA)

2. For each key FRUS document, perform a “parallel text hunt”:
   - Same date + same principals in presidential library finding aids
   - Same topic phrase in CIA Reading Room and NARA catalog
   - Same policy issue in congressional hearing material on GovInfo

3. Prioritize declassified finds that do one of three things:
   - Clarify decision timing
   - Expose options rejected from final policy
   - Illuminate verification intelligence judgments

4. Preserve linkable provenance in working notes:
   - URL
   - Collection name
   - Identifier/call number
   - Access date

This keeps draft annotations audit-ready and accelerates final citation cleanup.
